# Reading notes: Don't Make Me Think -A Common Sense Appraoch To Web Usability

Using common sense to solve UX problems and user availability

For usability, you don't need to know everything. Unless you are a usability professional, you don't have to learn everything about usability.

As a user, I should never have to think whether a button is clickable.

Every question mark addes to users' cognitive workload, distracting our attention from the task at hand. One distraction may be slight but when more than one distraction adds up, especially if it's something we do all the time like deciding what to click on. 

People don't like to puzzle over how to do things. 

Facts about real-world Web use
- We don't read pages. We scan them. 
- We don't make optimal choices. We satisfice.
- We don't figure out how things work. We muddle through


When you're testing websites, software, or household appliances, you're testing how people use things all the time without understanding how they work. (completely wrong-headed ideas about they work).

As for any sort of technology, very few people take the time to read instructions; people often forge ahead and muddle through, making up our own valuely plausible stories about what we're doing and why it works. 

Many people use the web extensively without knowing that they're using a browser. What they know is you type something in a box and stuff appears. But it doesn't matter to them: they just muddling through and using the thing successfully.

Muddling through is not limited to beginners. Even technically savvy users often have surprising gaps in their understanding of how things work.

## Billboard Design 101
Designing for scanning, not reading
- Take advantage of conventions
- Create effective visual hierarchies
- Break pages up into clearly defined areas
- Make it obvious what's clickable
- Eliminate distractions
- Format content to support scanning

The more importannt something is, the more prominent it is. 

Things that are related logically are related visually. 
Things are nested visually to show what's part of what.
